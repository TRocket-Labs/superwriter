# Concept Template

Use this template when developing the selected concept. The goal is to turn a promising candidate into a complete concept brief that a writer or strategist can use to plan multiple related pieces of content.

## Complete Concept Structure

```markdown
# [Concept Name]

## 1. Why It's Important

[Why should the audience learn this concept? What problem does understanding this concept solve?]

## 2. Scope

[What does this concept explain? What is intentionally outside its boundaries?]

## 3. Connection

[How does this concept naturally connect to the product, service, organization, or next action?]

## 4. Topics

[The individual blog posts, guides, tutorials, videos, or other pieces that together teach the concept.]

## Language Signals

[Audience language used to shape the concept.]
```

## Section Guidance

### 1. Why It's Important

Explain why the audience should understand this concept. Establish the value of learning it by explaining the problem it solves and what readers gain from understanding it.

Write 2-3 direct sentences:

1. Name the reader's situation or problem.
2. Explain what happens when the problem remains unresolved.
3. Close with what the concept helps the reader understand, decide, or do.

Keep the section focused on the reader's problem and learning payoff. It should not become an industry overview, a list of market problems, or a product pitch.

Questions to answer:

1. What problem does this concept help solve?
2. Why is this worth learning?
3. What changes once someone understands this concept?

### 2. Scope

Define the boundaries of the concept by describing the knowledge domain it covers. A good scope tells readers what they will understand after exploring the concept while making it clear what is intentionally outside its boundaries.

Write the scope as areas of understanding, not as a list of individual article topics.

Cover:

1. What the concept explains.
2. What decisions, workflows, problems, or tradeoffs belong inside it.
3. What adjacent subjects are outside the concept.

The section is done when a writer can judge whether a proposed topic belongs inside or outside the concept.

### 3. Connection

Explain how understanding this concept naturally leads someone to the product, service, organization, or next action.

The connection should feel like a logical next step from the reader's problem. Show where implementation, tooling, support, strategy, or deeper expertise becomes useful after the reader understands the concept.

Cover:

1. How this concept creates demand for action or implementation.
2. Where the product, service, organization, or next action fits into the adoption journey.
3. Why that connection follows from the concept instead of being attached as a sales message.

The section is done when the bridge from reader need to offer is clear without promotional language.

### 4. Topics

List the individual pieces of content that collectively teach the concept. Each topic should solve one specific problem or answer one focused question while contributing to the overall concept.

Together, the topics should progressively build understanding of the concept.

Organize topics by format when useful:

1. Blog posts: analysis, perspective, comparisons, arguments, or explanation.
2. Guides: goal-directed instructions for readers who already know the domain.
3. Tutorials: prescriptive walkthroughs for readers learning a tool, workflow, or method from the beginning.
4. Videos, workshops, emails, or other formats: use when the user's channel or audience calls for them.

Questions to answer:

1. What questions is the target audience asking in public discussions, search, sales calls, support conversations, or customer research?
2. What content already exists around this concept, and what gaps remain?
3. Does each topic contribute toward the same outcome as the others?

The section is done when every topic is distinct, useful on its own, and clearly part of the same concept.

### Language Signals

Show the research signals used to shape the concept. Include only signals that help explain the concept choice, naming, scope, or topics.

Use numbered groups:

1. Search phrases: exact terms or near-exact phrases people use when looking for help in this territory.
2. Community questions: questions practitioners ask in forums, social platforms, Q&A sites, comments, issues, or other public discussions.
3. Repeated problem language: recurring words, complaints, desired outcomes, tools, workflows, or alternatives the audience uses.

If live research was unavailable, label the signals as inferred from the user's context.

## Quality Checks

Before presenting the concept, check:

1. The concept is more than a bare phrase, keyword, or problem label.
2. The concept is broad enough to support several standalone pieces.
3. The concept is narrow enough that every topic contributes to the same shared problem, outcome, or thesis.
4. The scope defines boundaries instead of restating the topic list.
5. The connection follows naturally from the reader's need.
6. The topic list contains standalone pieces, not subheadings inside one article.
